Richard's Story

Hi, my name is Richard. Every day, I carry the hope of finding someone who might be willing to help me get a transplant. Right now, my health is okay, though I tire more easily than I used to, and the stress of waiting can feel heavy. At my age, waiting years for a deceased donor isn't a great option, so we're praying for a living donor who can give me the chance to look ahead with hope. Living donation is faster and leads to better outcomes, which is why I'm sharing my story with you today.

Getting a transplant would mean the world to me. I dream of a brighter future where I can live a more normal life and leave behind the worry of being tied to treatments like dialysis. If you or someone you know feels called to help, your generosity could truly extend my life. Even if donating isn't possible for you, sharing my story might connect me with the person who can help. Thank you for reading and for considering how you might be part of this life-changing journey.

Donor Eligibility Requirements

Richard is extremely lucky to have secured highly qualified medical help and support from the team at the University of Utah Health (UUH). He wants any interested persons to know that they will be in exceptional hands should they decide to step forward. UUH has shared the following as a pre-qualification list for donation:

  • Must be between the age of 18–69 years old.

  • Must be healthy and free of major health problems such as high blood pressure, diabetes, heart disease, obesity (BMI of 37 or less).

  • Must be mentally and emotionally healthy.

Questions & Answers

Family members are often the first considered for living donation due to potential compatibility. However, family members are not always a match or eligible to donate. Even if family has been tested, additional donors may still be needed. Anyone, including non-relatives, can be evaluated as a potential donor.

No, you do not need to have insurance to donate. The recipient's insurance typically covers the costs of the donor's evaluation, surgery, and immediate follow-up care. A GiftWorks Living Donor Navigator can walk you through the financial details specific to your situation.

Most living kidney donors are in the hospital for 2–3 days after surgery. Full recovery typically takes 2–4 weeks, depending on the individual. Many donors are able to return to desk jobs within a few weeks and resume full physical activity within 4–6 weeks.

Medical costs for the donor are typically covered by the recipient's insurance. Additionally, there are programs available to help reimburse donors for travel, lodging, and lost wages. Your Living Donor Navigator will help connect you with these resources so that finances are not a barrier to donating.

Donors must generally be between 18–69 years old, in good overall health, and free from conditions such as high blood pressure, diabetes, heart disease, or obesity (BMI 37 or less). They must also be mentally and emotionally healthy. A full medical evaluation will determine final eligibility.

If a living donor is not found, Richard would remain on the national deceased donor waiting list. The wait for a deceased donor kidney can be many years, and outcomes are generally not as favorable as with a living donor. This is why spreading awareness and finding a living donor is so critical.
